I trained as an opera singer, then moved into music education and nonprofit leadership. I co-direct the Colorado Institute of Music with my wife Leah, where most of what I do is the slow work of building small civic institutions: the kind that run on small staffs and committed boards, and don't last unless someone keeps showing up.

That work taught me something specific. When civic infrastructure exists, a community has somewhere to anchor things that need anchoring. When it doesn't, the things that needed anchoring drift, and trust gets cheap.

Why I'm building Image Trust Protocol

I've been working on a different civic gap. AI image generators got too cheap and too good. The 2024 to 2026 election cycle, the wars in Gaza and Ukraine, and the AI-generated and misattributed images that flooded coverage of Maduro's capture in Venezuela all surfaced the same problem. Image verification is civic infrastructure the internet doesn't have yet. Platforms can't be trusted to also be the arbiters of what's real on them. Newsrooms verify what reaches their desks; nothing covers the rest.

Someone has to build the layer that anchors truth when everything else fails. The layer has to be independent, auditable, and accountable, because the trustworthiness of attestations depends on the independence of the process that produces them.

That's what I'm building.

Image Trust Protocol is a verification API that synthesizes multi-source fact-check verdicts and returns them at the moment a question about an image or a claim needs an answer. The infrastructure underneath is open and auditable, the verdicts come from credentialed practitioners, and every verdict carries the publisher's attribution back to the source. Civic infrastructure underneath, a working business on top of it.
