Image verification is civic infrastructure the internet doesn't have yet.

by Mark Biesterfeld · Founder, Image Trust Protocol · Co-Executive Director, Colorado Institute of Music

AI image generators got too cheap and too good. The 2024 to 2026 election cycle, the wars in Gaza and Ukraine, and the AI-generated and misattributed images that flooded coverage of Maduro's capture in Venezuela all surfaced the same problem. Platforms can't be trusted to also be the arbiters of what's real on them. Newsrooms verify what reaches their desks; nothing covers the rest. Someone has to build the layer that anchors truth when everything else fails, and it has to be independent, auditable, and accountable, because the trustworthiness of attestations depends on the independence of the process that produces them.

I.

What I'm building

Image Trust Protocol is a verification API. Integrators (AI agents, browser extensions, content-provenance vendors, downstream AI products) call it when their users ask whether a photograph is real or whether a claim attached to it holds. The API returns a synthesized verdict: aggregated public fact-check data underneath, original verdicts from credentialed specialists on top, every record signed and recorded on a public ledger.

Specialist verdicts signed by credentialed practitioners, recorded on a public ledger
Synthesis consensus weighting, confidence score, machine-readable response
Aggregation public fact-check data, normalized and indexed

The infrastructure underneath is open and auditable. The verdicts come from specialists who do not work for the platform. Each verdict carries the publisher's attribution back to its source.

Civic infrastructure underneath, a working business on top of it.

II.

About me, and why I'm doing this

Mark Biesterfeld

I trained as an opera singer, then moved into music education and nonprofit leadership. I co-direct the Colorado Institute of Music with my wife Leah. Most of what I do there is the slow work of building small civic institutions: the kind that run on small staffs and committed boards, and don't last unless someone keeps showing up.

That work taught me something specific. When civic infrastructure exists, a community has somewhere to anchor the things that need anchoring. When it doesn't, those things drift, and trust gets cheap. Image verification is one of those things now. It needs an institution underneath it, and one doesn't exist yet.

I'm not an OSINT practitioner or a digital forensics specialist. My role is the organizer and builder. The verification work itself belongs to credentialed practitioners. My job is to give them a place to do it that lasts.
